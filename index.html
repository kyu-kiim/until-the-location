GPS IS

GPS stands for Global Positioning System, which is a system that calculates the current location using satellites. GPS receivers receive signals from several satellites orbiting the Earth and calculate where the user is on Earth. At this time, the location is usually expressed in coordinate values such as latitude, longitude, and altitude.

GPS requires information about the time spent on multiple satellites to calculate the location. Each satellite transmits the time it sent the signal and the location information together. Based on the time it took for the signal to arrive, the receiver calculates the distance to the satellite and estimates its location by comparing the distance to several satellites.

In general, GPS calculates locations using at least four satellite signals. Three satellites are used to locate space, and additional satellites are needed to correct the time error of the receiver. The more satellite signals you receive, the more stable and accurate your location calculation can be.

Our GPS always provides a more stable location.It does not rely solely on a single signal, but compares values from multiple satellites together. Unstable values are adjusted, and excessively bouncing errors are smoothly cleared in the mean.

Users don't need to know complicated calculations.GPS collects wobbly signals and suggests the most plausible location. Even if there is a slight variation from the actual location, the system continues to update the values and reduce errors.

Your position is being calibrated safely.

CREATE A REFERENCE POINT

Our GPS creates a reliable reference point to find the exact location. Each satellite transmits its location and time, and the receiver calculates its current location based on this information.

As soon as multiple reference points overlap, the user's location is organized into a single point.

This point doesn't happen by accident. It's a stable reference value created by satellite, time, distance, and calculation.

Your position is based on a safe standard.

NATURAL DISTURBANCE

GPS signals pass through Earth's atmosphere and reach users.In the process, natural factors such as ionosphere, troposphere, weather, humidity, topography, and trees can affect the signal.

Slow signals are calibrated, and weak signals are compared with values from other satellites. Nature can shake signals, but the system continues to find its most stable position.

Your location is estimated to be safe even in a changing environment.

ARTIFICIAL DISTURBANCE

Cities are complicated, but our GPS continues to work in them as well.High buildings, tunnels, underground spaces, and radio interference can weaken the signal. Sometimes the signal is reflected off the wall and calculated as if it had returned a longer way than it actually was.

But don't worry.

GPS detects these artificial disturbances and re-adjusts them to the extent possible. Shaking values are corrected, and uncertain signals are compared to other signals.

Your location is safely managed even in the city.

DATA CONVERSION

GPS doesn't stop at showing the coordinates as they are.Latitude and longitude become points on a map, which connect to roads, buildings, and destinations. One coordinate will soon be "current location," "nearby location," "remaining distance," and "arrival time."

The user does not have to interpret the numbers.GPS provides complex location data in everyday language. Difficult-to-know coordinates are organized into familiar location information, and movements are guided through easy-to-follow routes.

Your location is being converted into safe readable information.
